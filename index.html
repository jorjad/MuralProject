The Cuban Missile Crisis: Digital Museums

Accessing information through museum websites from opposing sides of the Cuban Missile Crisis. 

When we do research about historical events, how do we acquire information specific to one country's experience? What accessible resources do we as researchers look to when we attempt to understand a nation's perspective? What barriers do we encounter, and why are those barriers in place?

How do we shape our contextual understanding?

To understand why researchers encounter barriers whilst researching an event such as the Cuban Missile Crisis, the context must be understood. The following TedX Talk video briefly describes the context of the event.

    The Russian Perspective

    How does a Russian Museum website portray the Cuban Missile Crisis?

    When searching for a Russian museum with an exhibit on the Cuban Missile Crisis online, the only results that populate are North American websites. When the search is broadened to a Cold War Museum, eventually the Bunker-42 museum website populates. 

    The B-42 Museum


    The Bunker 42 Museum has seven tabs within the webpage: history, tours, prices, a photo gallery, patriotic education program information, social support from their sponsors, and contact information. 


    The history page explains the construction of the bunker itself rather than the purpose and need for the bunker. 

    Why do the two countries differ in their distribution of digital information?

    As we conduct research on the internet and encounter barriers such as the inability to find up to date, relevant information about nations such as Russia we must think critically about the social, economic, and political dynamics of the internet.
